## Act II: The Geometry of Ethics

When there are multiple goals (health, wealth, relationships, meaning...), you face **trade-offs**.

All Pareto optimal points form **Pareto frontier**—this is a geometric surface.

**Ethical decisions are choosing a point on Pareto frontier.**

Wrong method: **Euclidean gradient** (straight-line climbing)

$$
\tilde{\nabla} f = g^{-1} \nabla f
$$

Natural gradient considers space curvature, is **fastest ascent direction in information geometry**.

**Moral "ought" is direction of natural gradient—path fastest increasing survival ability under given constraints.**

### Risk Consistency

**Risk Consistency**:

- Ensures transitivity of decisions (if A>B and B>C, then A>C)

Value functions satisfying this constraint form a **convex cone**—this is geometric constraint of ethics.

**Not all preferences are reasonable—only risk-consistent ones can form stable ethics.**

**Meaning is "how far is the way home"—information cost of returning to viable survival region.**

## Act VII: Semantic Curvature

Meaning has not only distance, but also **curvature**.

### Definition

$$
\mathcal{R}_{\text{semantic}} = \text{Riemann curvature of } (\mathcal{M}, g)
$$

Curvature measures "degree of bending" of space.

**Positive curvature** (sphere):
- Paths converge
- Synergistic effects (1+1>2)
- Meaning amplification

**Negative curvature** (saddle):
- Paths diverge
- Conflicting effects (1+1<2)
- Meaning dilution

**Zero curvature** (plane):
- Paths parallel
- Independent effects (1+1=2)

### Synergy vs. Conflict

**Meaning dilution—whole less than sum of parts.**

**Life wisdom is finding positive curvature regions—letting multiple values reinforce each other.**
